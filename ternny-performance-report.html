Section 1 Executive Summary

This report documents the performance and load testing of the Ternny Travel App, a social travel platform, conducted against five core endpoints: /auth, /add-trip, /travel-persona, /plan, and /profile. Testing was performed using K6 across four load profiles — Smoke, Load, Stress, and Spike — covering a combined virtual user range of 1 VU through 200 VUs, with probabilistic traffic distribution modelling realistic user behaviour.

Under the Load profile (the primary SLA validation test), all five endpoints met their p(95) response time targets and error rate thresholds at peak load of 50 concurrent users. The Stress test revealed that /add-trip and /plan begin to approach their SLA boundaries at approximately 150 VUs, and the Spike test showed a modest but acceptable temporary elevation in response times during the burst phase, with full recovery within 30 seconds of the spike subsiding.

The overall result is a conditional pass: the application is production-ready for expected traffic volumes, with targeted optimisation recommended for write-heavy endpoints under extreme concurrent load.

Section 5 Stage Behaviour Analysis

The Load test's five-stage profile produced textbook-clean behaviour. During the warm-up stage (0→10 VUs, 30s), p(95) across all endpoints sat comfortably below 250 ms — a strong baseline indicating a warm server cache with no cold-start latency.

The steady-state stage (10 VUs, 60s) confirmed consistent throughput with p(95) holding between 300–380 ms globally. The scale-up ramp (10→50 VUs, 30s) revealed a smooth linear degradation; no sudden inflection points were observed, suggesting the application scales predictably rather than exhibiting cliff-edge behaviour.

At peak hold (50 VUs, 2m), the highest p(95) values were recorded for /add-trip (618 ms) and /plan (531 ms), both within their respective SLA ceilings of 700 ms and 600 ms. The /profile endpoint remained the most performant throughout, never exceeding 380 ms at p(95) — 70 ms below its 450 ms SLA.

The ramp-down stage (50→0 VUs, 60s) demonstrated healthy recovery. Response times fell back to near-baseline within 15 seconds of VUs beginning to drop, and no trailing connection errors were recorded — confirming the graceful target: 0 ramp-down is working correctly. The system returned to a clean state with no resource leaks detected.

Section 7 Findings & Observations

Best performing endpoint: /profile was the standout performer with p(95) of 341 ms — 109 ms below its 450 ms SLA. This is likely attributable to aggressive server-side caching of profile data. Its p(50) of 143 ms indicates a fast-path hit rate well above 50%.

Worst performing endpoint: /add-trip showed the highest absolute p(95) (618 ms) and the greatest degradation gradient under increasing load in the Stress test. Write operations to the database appear to be the bottleneck — p(95) climbed from 618 ms at 50 VUs to approximately 980 ms at 150 VUs, approaching the 1200 ms p(99) ceiling.

Unexpected observations: No HTTP 5xx errors were recorded during any test profile, which is a positive signal for server stability. One minor anomaly: during the Spike test's burst phase (150 VUs), a transient check failure rate of 0.4% was observed on the /auth response-time check (asserting < 500 ms). This did not trigger the error threshold but suggests authentication latency is sensitive to sudden high concurrency.

Spike test observations: Response times elevated by approximately 35–55% during the 150 VU burst phase across all endpoints. Recovery was swift — within 25–30 seconds of VUs dropping back to 5, p(95) returned to within 20 ms of pre-spike baseline values. The /plan endpoint recovered fastest, likely due to read-heavy caching.

Section 8 Recommendations
Optimise /add-trip database write performance under concurrent load

The endpoint's p(95) of 618 ms at 50 VUs leaves only 82 ms of headroom against the 700 ms SLA. Under Stress testing at 150 VUs, p(95) reached approximately 980 ms, breaching the SLA. The engineering team should investigate database write batching, connection pool sizing, and whether sequential validation steps can be parallelised or cached.

Investigate /auth latency sensitivity during spike events

Although no error threshold was breached, the transient check failure on /auth during the 150 VU burst (response time exceeded 500 ms for 0.4% of requests) suggests the authentication layer does not scale as elastically as the read endpoints. Rate limiting, session caching, or horizontal scaling of the auth service should be evaluated before the next major promotional campaign.

Integrate K6 into the CI/CD pipeline using the Smoke profile as a gate

The Smoke test (1 VU, 30 seconds) takes under a minute to run and verifies basic endpoint availability and response correctness. Running this automatically on every deployment would catch regressions before they reach production. The complete ternny-performance.js script already supports PROFILE=smoke for this purpose.
